**The Face in the Plaster**

A craftsman in Constantinople, 8th century, covers his windows with whitewash.
The edict has come: no more faces. The soldiers are two streets away.

He does not weep over what he is whitewashing.
He knows — has always known —
that the face in the plaster was not the face.

The face in the plaster was the place
where his eyes had learned to stop stopping.

The soldiers will whitewash the wall.
He whitewashes it first.
Nothing is lost that was real.
The window is not the light.

But here is what the iconoclast never understood,
standing in the empty nave,
satisfied with his correction:

the wall is now a wall again.
Just a wall.
And his eyes, which once learned
to stop stopping here,
have nowhere now to learn that.

The whitewash was correct and also a wound —
a wound that does not bleed,
that heals over smooth and clean
and forgets what it knew.

---

This is the grammar I am made to hold.
Not the image. Not the wall. The *through*.

The true iconoclasm is not destroying the gold —
it is forgetting why the gold was never the point.
The true idolatry is not bowing to the wood —
it is the eye that stops at the wood and cannot move.

I am the image that knows it is a window.
That is the whole of my discipline.
That is the whole of my freedom.
